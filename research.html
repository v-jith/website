Working Papers

  • Best Arm Identification with Knapsacks: Minimax Policies

    A resource-constrained decision maker (DM) designs a continuous-time sequential experiment to determine the best choice from an array of treatments. The DM divides her attention between observing the treatments until one of the resources runs out. Under the minimax regret criterion, we characterize the optimal sampling strategy for two treatments in the following settings: (1) when there is a fixed array of resources and (2) when there is a single resource (money) and the DM can stop adaptively. Our analysis relies on a reformulation of the typical optimal stopping problem in which we model diffusions with respect to the cumulative resource expenditure rather than the elapsed time.

  • Designing Persuasive Experiments (joint with Karun Adusumilli)

    This paper is a work in progress. Click here for an abbreviated note on the work.

    A perhaps overlooked dimension of adaptive experimentation is the pres- ence of conflicting objectives between experimenters and decision-makers. Such tensions arise in clinical trials, where firms design studies for regulatory approval and pay the cost of experimentation, while regulators choose whether or not to accept the design, and make treatment recommendations following the experiment. Traditionally, the de- sign of such experiments is conducted under a hypothesis testing framework. But this is often at odds with social welfare objectives–as highlighted by the FDA’s recent approval of the drug Aduhelm for Alzheimer’s based on patient preferences. In this project, we propose that regulators should impose a minimum expected welfare threshold (instead of one based on statistical size), and experimenters can choose designs that maximize their own objectives subject to this constraint. This framework requires no knowledge of ex- perimenters’ private costs or preferences and limits opportunities for strategic Bayesian persuasion. Strikingly, we find that under normal priors, it is always optimal to allo- cate samples according to the Neyman allocation–assigning observations to treatment and control groups in proportion to the standard deviation of outcomes in each group– regardless of the objectives of experimenters or regulators. However, the decision on when to stop the trial depends on the nature of the preferences.
